== Preface

=== On Patterns and Why Engineers Struggle with Them

Most software engineers first encounter design patterns in a university course.
You build a toy project, memorize some UML diagrams, implement a Singleton to
satisfy a homework assignment, and move on. The projects are too small for the
patterns to matter — there is no team to coordinate with, no codebase that has
outgrown one person's comprehension, no production system where a bad
abstraction means six months of rework. Patterns feel like ceremony in that
context: verbose, overly formal, solutions looking for problems.

I know this because I lived it. When I first encountered pattern catalogs, I
found them archaic. The class diagrams felt like relics of an era obsessed with
inheritance hierarchies. The prose was dense. I could not connect any of it to
the code I was actually writing. For years I dismissed the whole idea.

**What changed my mind was scale.** When the scope of a system exceeds what you can
hold in your head — seven services, four teams, a timeline measured in
quarters — you need a way to communicate structural ideas quickly. Not
implementation details, but the _shape_ of a solution, to people who will go
build their piece of it independently. A three-page design document explaining
how your system coordinates distributed actions with paired rollbacks that
unwind in reverse order on failure takes a meeting, a whiteboard session, and
three rounds of review comments to land. Saying "this is a Saga" and pointing
to a well-documented pattern **communicates the same thing in ten seconds**.

In a presentation I gave on engineering communication, I argued that diagrams
are one of the most underused tools in our profession. Civil, mechanical,
electrical, and architectural engineers all use shared diagrams as their primary
medium for planning and review. Research backs this up: people skim text in
https://www.nngroup.com/articles/f-shaped-pattern-reading-web-content/[F-shaped patterns^] (Nielsen Norman Group), but they engage with diagrams holistically. Diagrams reduce ambiguity, force precision, and make hidden
assumptions visible. Patterns serve the same function at a higher level of
abstraction. A diagram communicates the structure of one system; a pattern
communicates the structure of an entire _class_ of systems. When a team shares
a pattern vocabulary, they can reason about architecture the way structural
engineers reason about trusses — by naming the form, not describing each beam.

=== How I Discovered Durable Execution

I spent a large part of my career in financial services, where workflows are
fundamental. Transaction processing, compliance checks, loan origination,
payment reconciliation — these are long-running stateful processes that must
survive failures, handle human intervention, and maintain audit trails. Every
company in the space builds some version of a workflow engine, whether they call
it that or not.

When I became CTO and co-founder of a fintech startup in 2016, we built the
first version of our platform on a Java workflow engine from the BPM world.
Business logic lived in BPMN XML files — diagrams that compiled into execution
graphs. The engine ran inside a servlet container, and you could not test a
workflow without standing up the full stack: application server, database, the
engine itself. There was no way to write a unit test that exercised just the
process logic. The engine exposed a REST API, but there were no language SDKs
outside Java — which meant every non-Java service had to talk to it through raw
HTTP calls with no type safety, no IDE support, and no way to catch contract
changes at compile time. In practice, it was a tool designed for the J2EE era:
perfectly at home inside an enterprise monolith, deeply foreign to a polyglot
microservice architecture. The engine worked well for what it was designed to do,
but when the business needed behavior that BPMN could not express natively,
you had to reach for escape hatches: custom Java delegates, REST calls that
returned routing decisions, or the external task service pattern. All
workable, but each one pulled you further from the visual process model
that was supposed to be the point.

We eventually replaced it with a choreography-based architecture: services
communicating through events, each owning its own slice of the business process.
This solved the monolith problem but introduced a different set of failures.
Observability disappeared overnight — no single place showed the state of a
loan application as it moved through underwriting, compliance, and disbursement.
Error handling became inconsistent across teams. One team would retry failed
steps; another would dead-letter them; a third would silently drop them and
rely on a daily reconciliation batch to catch the gaps. Compensating actions
were implemented ad hoc, and the subtle bugs they introduced surfaced months
later in production, usually during an audit.

When I discovered durable execution, it collapsed the gap between what I wanted
to express and what the platform could support. **Write sequential code, and the
runtime handles persistence, retries, and failure recovery.** The state lives in
the code, not in a database schema you maintain separately. **The workflow _is_
the program.** I had spent years building infrastructure to paper over the
distance between business logic and execution guarantees. Durable execution
**simply removed that distance**.

=== Who Is This Book For?

This book is for **backend engineers and architects** who build distributed
systems — whether you're already using a durable execution platform or
evaluating one. You should be comfortable reading code in at least one of
Go, Java, Python, TypeScript, or C#. Familiarity with concepts like retries,
idempotency, and eventual consistency will help, but the chapters explain what
you need as they go.

If you've never heard of durable execution, the Introduction provides the
context. If you're already building with Temporal or a similar platform, the
pattern catalog gives you a vocabulary for the recurring designs you've likely
encountered but haven't named.

=== What This Book Is (and Is Not)

This book is about **durable execution _patterns_**, not about any specific
platform.

A durable execution platform lets you build things that are genuinely difficult
without one: long-running stateful processes, distributed coordination, failure
recovery, exactly-once semantics. These capabilities enable recurring structural
solutions — patterns — that are worth naming, documenting, and reusing.

The examples use https://temporal.io[Temporal]. I am a member of the
https://temporal.io/community#702702[Temporal Constellation program], and I
chose Temporal because it is the most flexible and complete implementation of
the durable execution model available today. Each chapter uses a different
Temporal SDK language — Go, Java, Python, TypeScript, .NET — to show that these
patterns are not language-specific.

The patterns themselves are platform-agnostic. Other durable execution
platforms — Restate, DBOS, Azure Durable Functions, Infinitic, and others —
make different design decisions and support different subsets of these patterns.
Some patterns require capabilities — such as external messaging into workflows, synchronous state inspection, and history compaction — that not every platform provides. The consequences section of each pattern discusses
these constraints honestly.

For an up-to-date list of platforms, I maintain
https://github.com/edmondop/awesome-durable-executions[Awesome Durable Executions],
an open-source catalog of platforms, tools, and resources in this space.

I hope this book gives you a vocabulary that makes your distributed systems
clearer to design, easier to communicate about, and less painful to operate.
